For this specific scaling of the KdV equation, we define the operators $\hat{L}$ and $\hat{H}$ as follows: 

1. The spatial operator: $\hat{L} = -6\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} -v$

$$\hat{L}(\hat{H}\psi) = -6\partial^2\left(-4\psi_{xxx} - v\psi_x - \frac{1}{2}v_x\psi\right) - v\left(-4\psi_{xxx} - v\psi_x - \frac{1}{2}v_x\psi\right)$$

Finally, substitute our results from Step 1 and Step 5 back into your original Lax equation:

$$\frac{\partial \hat{L}}{\partial t} - (\hat{H}\hat{L} - \hat{L}\hat{H}) = -v_t - \frac{1}{2}vv_x - v_{xxx} = 0$$

Rearranging this gives us the KdV equation:
$$v_t + vv_x + v_{xxx} = 0$$

## The Big Picture

The KdV equation is:
$$\frac{\partial v}{\partial t} - 6v\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial^3 v}{\partial x^3} = 0$$

The **hidden linear equation** (the Lax pair trick) is that KdV can be mapped to a Schrödinger equation:
$$-\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} + v(x,0)\psi = E\psi$$

Each **bound state** ($E_i < 0$) of this Schrödinger equation produces one soliton in the time evolution, with amplitude:
$$U_i = \frac{2}{3}|E_i| \quad \text{(soliton height)}$$

## Step 1: Find Bound States of the Schrödinger Equation

For a **single attractive delta function** $-\alpha\delta(x)$, the bound state has:
$$E = -\frac{\alpha^2}{4}$$

For **two delta functions** $v = -\alpha[\delta(x-a) + \delta(x+a)]$, we solve by matching boundary conditions.

### Ansatz (symmetric case, even parity):

$$\psi(x) = \begin{cases} e^{\kappa x} & x < -a \\ A\cosh(\kappa x) & -a < x < a \\ e^{-\kappa x} & x > a \end{cases}$$

where $\kappa = \sqrt{-E} > 0$.

### Boundary conditions at $x = a$:

Continuity of $\psi$: $e^{-\kappa a} = A\cosh(\kappa a)$

Discontinuity of $\psi'$ (from delta function):
$$\psi'(a^+) - \psi'(a^-) = \alpha\psi(a)$$
$$-\kappa e^{-\kappa a} - A(-\kappa\sinh(\kappa a)) \cdot (-1) = ... $$

Applying both conditions gives the **transcendental equation for even states**:

$$\boxed{\kappa = \alpha\left(1 + e^{-2\kappa a}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\tanh(\kappa a) \cdot \text{(see below)}}}$$

Let me write this cleanly. The matching gives:

$$\kappa = \alpha(1 + e^{-2\kappa a}) \quad \text{(even parity)}$$
$$\kappa = \alpha(1 - e^{-2\kappa a}) \quad \text{(odd parity)}$$

---## Step 2: The Transcendental Equations — Derivation

For the potential $v = -\alpha[\delta(x-a) + \delta(x+a)]$ with $\kappa = \sqrt{-E}$, the wavefunction is:

$$\psi(x) = \begin{cases} e^{\kappa x} & x < -a \\ A\cosh(\kappa x) \text{ or } B\sinh(\kappa x) & -a < x < a \\ e^{-\kappa x} & x > a \end{cases}$$

The delta function boundary condition $[\psi']_{x=a} = \alpha\,\psi(a)$ combined with continuity gives:

$$\boxed{\kappa = \alpha(1 + e^{-2\kappa a})} \quad \text{(even parity)}$$
$$\boxed{\kappa = \alpha(1 - e^{-2\kappa a})} \quad \text{(odd parity)}$$

Key observations:
- The **even state always exists** (the right-hand side starts at $2\alpha > 0$ and decreases to $\alpha$, always crossing the line $\kappa$)
- The **odd state exists only when** $\alpha a > 1/2$ (the slope condition at origin)
- Each solution gives $E_i = -\kappa_i^2$ and one soliton

## Step 3: Solitons from Bound States

Each bound state energy $E_i < 0$ produces a KdV soliton with:

$$U_i = \frac{2}{3}|E_i| = \frac{2}{3}\kappa_i^2 \quad \text{(amplitude)}$$

The soliton's speed is $c_i = U_i$ (faster = taller), and the full time-evolved solution separates into individual solitons traveling to the right at different speeds.

## Step 4: The Full Inverse Scattering Picture

The IST method works in three steps:

1. **Forward scattering** at $t=0$: solve the Schrödinger equation, find $\{E_i, \kappa_i\}$ (discrete) and reflection/transmission coefficients $R(k), T(k)$ (continuous spectrum)

2. **Time evolution** of scattering data: the bound state norming constants evolve as $c_i(t) = c_i(0)\,e^{4\kappa_i^3 t}$, while $R(k,t) = R(k,0)\,e^{8ik^3t}$

3. **Inverse scattering** (Gel'fand–Levitan–Marchenko equation): reconstruct $v(x,t)$ from the evolved scattering data

For a pure **reflectionless** potential (which delta functions approximate for the soliton part), the reconstruction simplifies to:
$$v(x,t) = -2\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\ln\det(I + A)$$
where $A$ is a matrix built from the norming constants.

Solution:

Here's the full step-by-step solution. This is a classic inverse scattering problem for the KdV equation.

---

## Problem setup

The KdV equation is:

$$u_t - 6uu_x + u_{xxx} = 0$$

Given the initial condition $v(x) = \delta(a-x) + \delta(a+x)$, we need to find solitons by analyzing the Schrödinger eigenvalue problem associated with the inverse scattering transform.

---

## Step 1: The Schrödinger scattering problem

The IST for KdV uses the **time-independent Schrödinger equation** (Lax pair):

$$-\psi_{xx} + v(x)\psi = \lambda\psi, \qquad \lambda = -\kappa^2$$

Bound states (with $\lambda < 0$) correspond to **solitons**. So we need to find the **negative eigenvalues** of:

$$-\psi_{xx} + [\delta(a-x) + \delta(a+x)]\psi = -\kappa^2\psi$$

---

## Step 2: Piecewise solution in three regions

The potential is zero everywhere except at $x = \pm a$. Define three regions:

| Region | Range |
|--------|-------|
| I | $x < -a$ |
| II | $-a < x < a$ |
| III | $x > a$ |

In each region, the equation is $\psi'' = \kappa^2 \psi$, so:

$$\psi = \begin{cases} A e^{\kappa x} & x < -a \\ B e^{\kappa x} + C e^{-\kappa x} & -a < x < a \\ D e^{-\kappa x} & x > a \end{cases}$$

(Exponentially decaying at $\pm\infty$ for bound states.)


How this conclusion was reached in detail. Full derivation:

In region I ($x < -a$), the solution must decay as $x \to -\infty$, so we take $\psi = A e^{\kappa x}$.
In region II ($-a < x < a$), the general solution is a linear combination of exponentials: $\psi = B e^{\kappa x} + C e^{-\kappa x}$.
In region III ($x > a$), the solution must decay as $x \to +\infty$, so we take $\psi = D e^{-\kappa x}$.


---

## Step 3: Matching conditions at the delta functions

At each delta function $\delta(x - x_0)$, the matching conditions are:
- **Continuity**: $\psi$ is continuous
- **Derivative jump**: $\psi'$ jumps by $+\psi(x_0)$

**At $x = -a$:**

$$\psi \text{ continuous:} \quad Ae^{-\kappa a} = Be^{-\kappa a} + Ce^{\kappa a} \tag{1}$$

$$\psi' \text{ jump:} \quad \kappa Be^{-\kappa a} - \kappa Ce^{\kappa a} - \kappa Ae^{-\kappa a} = -Ae^{-\kappa a} \tag{2}$$

From (2): $\kappa(B e^{-\kappa a} - Ce^{\kappa a} - Ae^{-\kappa a}) = -Ae^{-\kappa a}$

**At $x = +a$:**

$$Be^{\kappa a} + Ce^{-\kappa a} = De^{-\kappa a} \tag{3}$$

$$-\kappa De^{-\kappa a} - \kappa Be^{\kappa a} + \kappa Ce^{-\kappa a} = -De^{-\kappa a} \tag{4}$$

---

## Step 4: Exploiting symmetry (even/odd modes)

Since the potential $v(x) = \delta(a-x)+\delta(a+x)$ is **symmetric** under $x \to -x$, solutions split into:

**Even modes:** $D = A$, $C = B$

**Odd modes:** $D = -A$, $C = -B$

### Even mode

From (1): $Ae^{-\kappa a} = Be^{-\kappa a} + Be^{\kappa a} = B(e^{-\kappa a} + e^{\kappa a})$

$$\Rightarrow A = B(1 + e^{2\kappa a}) \tag{5}$$

Substituting into (2):

$$\kappa(Be^{-\kappa a} - Be^{\kappa a} - Ae^{-\kappa a}) = -Ae^{-\kappa a}$$

$$\kappa B(e^{-\kappa a} - e^{\kappa a}) - \kappa Ae^{-\kappa a} = -Ae^{-\kappa a}$$

Using (5), divide through by $Be^{-\kappa a}$:

$$-2\kappa\sinh(\kappa a) - \kappa(1 + e^{2\kappa a}) = -(1 + e^{2\kappa a})$$

Rearranging:

$$\boxed{\kappa = 1 - \kappa\tanh(\kappa a)} \quad \text{(even mode)}$$

Or equivalently:

$$\kappa(1 + \tanh(\kappa a)) = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \kappa \cdot \frac{e^{\kappa a} + e^{-\kappa a} + e^{\kappa a} - e^{-\kappa a}}{e^{\kappa a} + e^{-\kappa a}} = 1$$

$$\Rightarrow \quad 2\kappa e^{\kappa a} = e^{\kappa a} + e^{-\kappa a} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{\kappa = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + e^{-2\kappa a}\right)}$$

### Odd mode

By the same procedure with $C = -B$:

$$\boxed{\kappa = 1 + \kappa\tanh(\kappa a)} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \kappa = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - e^{-2\kappa a}\right)$$

---

## Step 5: Counting and solving the eigenvalue equations

Define $f(\kappa) = \kappa - \frac{1}{2}(1 + e^{-2\kappa a})$ for the **even** mode. Note:
- $f(0) = -\frac{1}{2}(1+1) = -1 < 0$
- As $\kappa \to \infty$, $f \to +\infty$

So there is **always exactly one even bound state**, for any $a > 0$.

For the **odd** mode, $g(\kappa) = \kappa - \frac{1}{2}(1 - e^{-2\kappa a})$:
- $g(0) = 0$
- $g'(0) = 1 - \frac{1}{2}(1 + 0) \cdot ... $

More carefully: $g'(0) = 1 - ae^{-2\kappa a}|_{\kappa=0} \cdot (-1)$... let's compute directly:

$$g(\kappa) = \kappa - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}e^{-2\kappa a}$$
$$g'(\kappa) = 1 - ae^{-2\kappa a}$$

At $\kappa=0$: $g(0) = 0 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 0$ and $g'(0) = 1 - a$.

- If $a > 1$: $g'(0) < 0$, so $g$ dips below zero for small $\kappa > 0$, meaning there is **one odd bound state**.
- If $a \leq 1$: $g'(0) \geq 0$ and $g$ stays positive, so **no odd bound state**.

---

## Step 6: The solitons

Each bound state with eigenvalue $\lambda = -\kappa^2$ gives one **soliton** of KdV:

$$u(x,t) = -2\kappa^2 \,\mathrm{sech}^2\!\left(\kappa(x - 4\kappa^2 t) - \delta\right)$$

where $\delta$ encodes the initial position (from the norming constants).

| Case | Number of solitons | Condition |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| $a \leq 1$ | **1 soliton** | Even mode only |
| $a > 1$ | **2 solitons** | Even + odd mode |

The soliton amplitudes are $2\kappa_{1,2}^2$ and their speeds are $4\kappa_{1,2}^2$, where $\kappa_{1,2}$ solve the respective transcendental equations above.

---

## Summary

The eigenvalue equations to solve are:

$$\text{Even: } \quad e^{-2\kappa_e a} = 2\kappa_e - 1$$

$$\text{Odd: } \quad e^{-2\kappa_o a} = 1 - 2\kappa_o \quad (\text{only if } a > 1)$$

Each solution $\kappa > 0$ maps to a one-soliton solution. Let me show this graphically:The blue line $y = \kappa$ always intersects the even-mode curve (left panel) — hence always one even soliton. ## Final answer

The initial condition $v(x) = \delta(a-x)+\delta(a+x)$ produces:

- **1 soliton** when $0 < a \leq 1$, with amplitude $2\kappa_e^2$ where $\kappa_e$ solves $e^{-2\kappa a} = 2\kappa - 1$
- **2 solitons** when $a > 1$, with a second (slower, lower) soliton of amplitude $2\kappa_o^2$ where $\kappa_o$ solves $e^{-2\kappa a} = 1 - 2\kappa$
